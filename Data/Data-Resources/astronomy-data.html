Citizen Science opportunities

Web-based projects

Originally grabbed from here, which has a much better (if possibly dated) list, with a description for each project.

These programs all involve the use of real data on the forefront of astronomical research, but are "packaged" so as to make accessing the real data far more easy (e.g., entirely web browser-based) than it might be otherwise. These projects are a great way to start to get into real data. Many offer "hooks" to get into the research questions at a deeper level when you are ready. Many have lesson plans ready-to-go -- by which I mean, lessons you can do yourself, or if you are an educator, you can use in a classroom or after school setting. Some may have shut down while others have started.

  • Zooniverse has a TON -- go to the list of all Zooniverse projects, and scroll through the list to find the astronomy ones.
  • SETI at home
  • International Observer the Moon Night
  • Globe at Night. NB: data will also be used for evaluating effects on wildlife; activities to come.
  • Great World-Wide Star Count
  • Solar Stormwatch
  • Stardust at Home
  • Citizen Sky
  • Cosmo Quest
  • Some citizen science programs are available at the Pisgah Astronomical Research Institute - see links on that page.

Major US Astronomy Archives

  • IRSA, the Infrared Science Archive (this is the one at Caltech) - NASA's IR and sub-mm data.
  • NED, the NASA Extragalactic Database (also at Caltech) - curated data about galaxies ("google for galaxies")
  • The NASA Exoplanet Archive (also at Caltech) - curated data about exoplanets
  • MAST, the Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes (at STScI in Baltimore) - NASA's optical data
  • HEASARC, the High Energy Astrophysics Science Archive Research Center (at Goddard in Greenbelt, MD) - NASA's high-energy data
  • ADS, the Astrophysics Data System (at Harvard) - all the astronomy literature from the 1800s through this morning.
There are other archives for planetary data (there are several); and archives housing data from Canada, Europe, India, and Asia assets ... Two of the most famous archives are based in France: SIMBAD and VizieR. SIMBAD is mostly object-driven, e.g., type in an object's name or RA/Dec and find out what is known about that object, with links to journal articles. VizieR gives you fast access to ~16,000 different published data tables.
